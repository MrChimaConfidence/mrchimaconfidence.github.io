01 Overview

The Platform

Three personas. One system.

Chimreff connects Lagos customers with skilled artisans for device and appliance repairs — with rider pickup, live tracking, and delivery built in. Each of the three user types has its own onboarding, dashboard, and core task flow.

The Brief

Evaluate before launch.

I was brought in to run two structured rounds of heuristic evaluation across all three user flows before go-live — identifying friction points, inconsistencies, and drop-off risks before real users encountered them.

The Outcome

21 findings, all resolved.

Round 1 surfaced 21 findings including 5 high-severity issues. By Round 2, all critical issues were resolved. The platform entered a launch-ready state, with research directly shaping the phased go-to-market strategy.

02 Platform Scope

Why this matters

Most repair platforms serve one user type. Chimreff's three-persona model — with role-specific auth, onboarding, dashboards, and task flows — meant every design decision had to work across fundamentally different mental models and goals.

High

No onboarding guidance for new artisans

A new artisan completing their profile had no indication of what to do next. No progress checklist, no prompt. A significant drop-off risk on the platform's most critical persona.

High

Bio field with no guidance

The bio field was blank with no example or placeholder. Artisans didn't know what to write or how it affects their visibility to customers.

High

GPS error state too alarming on first load

Riders were immediately confronted with multiple red error messages about location permission — creating unnecessary anxiety before any context was given.

High

Conflicting 'Live' badge when GPS is denied

The header showed a green 'Live' badge even when location permission was denied — a direct contradiction that undermines trust in the system's reliability.

High

Dashboard gives no direction to new users

A new artisan with all zeros sees no guidance. No getting-started checklist, no prompt to complete availability. The dashboard was completely silent.

Mod

Commission section uses alarming language

'What You Owe (Personal Account Payments)' in orange created a sense of debt even at zero balance. Recommended reword: 'Commission Summary'.

Mod

Trust signals missing on homepage

The platform's operational history was never mentioned — a missed opportunity to differentiate from newer startups and build immediate credibility.

Full breakdown: Homepage (4) · Sign In (1) · Artisan Profile (7) · Artisan Dashboard (5) · Rider Dashboard (4)

Key Insight

Users weren't struggling to use the app. They were struggling to feel guided by it.

Launch Decision

All 5 HIGH severity findings were resolved by Round 2. The platform entered a launch-ready state with a small polish backlog — none of which were blockers. Remaining items were scoped as a focused post-launch fix pass.

05 Onboarding Tours

The Solution

A contextual onboarding tour for each user type — anchored to the real UI.

Artisan Tour — 5 Steps

Workspace intro → setup checklist → job management → profile & rate → ready to earn

Rider Tour — 4 Steps

Dashboard intro → pickups overview → queue management → ready to ride

Why it works

Each modal is anchored to the actual part of the screen it explains — users see the real UI in context, not abstract illustrations. 'Skip tour' respects autonomy, and a persistent help button means no user is ever permanently lost.

06 Launch Strategy

Core Principle

Supply before demand. Recruit artisans and riders first. A single failed job in Lagos creates lasting damage to word-of-mouth. Build a reliable supply base before spending a single kobo on customer acquisition.

01

Build the supply base

Weeks 1–3

Onboard 30+ active artisans and riders before any customer campaign launches. Field recruitment at Computer Village (Ikeja), Alaba International Market, and AC/appliance repairers in Surulere. WhatsApp community, video testimonials from early sign-ups.

02

Soft launch & stress test

Weeks 4–5

Complete 30–50 real jobs before public launch. Invite trusted contacts, monitor every job manually, log every issue — bugs, slow response times, artisan no-shows. Fix before going public. Request honest feedback from every test user.

03

Customer-side launch

Weeks 6–8

Acquire first 100 paying customers through TikTok/Instagram Reels, Meta paid ads targeting Lagos residents aged 22–45, and estate WhatsApp community outreach in Lekki, Ajah, Ikeja GRA, Yaba, and Victoria Island.

04

Rider growth & retention

Ongoing from Week 5

Build a self-sustaining rider network through referral bonuses, monthly leaderboards with cash prizes, and weekly earnings summaries via WhatsApp. Community milestones celebrated to build loyalty at zero cost.

Key metrics tracked weekly: Job Completion Rate (target 90%+) · Average Response Time (target under 30 mins) · Customer Repeat Rate · Cost Per Booking

Two structured rounds of heuristic evaluation identified and resolved all critical usability risks before launch.
