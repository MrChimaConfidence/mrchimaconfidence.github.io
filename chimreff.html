Overview

Chimreff is a Lagos-based repair-tech platform connecting customers with skilled artisans for device and appliance repairs — with rider pickup, live tracking, and delivery built in. The platform serves three distinct user types: Customers booking repairs, Artisans managing jobs and availability, and Riders handling pickup and delivery logistics.

As the Product Designer, I was responsible for evaluating the full platform before launch — testing all three user flows across two structured rounds of heuristic evaluation, then translating research findings into a phased go-to-market strategy.

Platform Scope

Three user types. Three distinct flows. Each with its own onboarding, dashboard, and core tasks.

Why this matters: Most repair platforms serve one user type. Chimreff's three-persona model — with role-specific auth, onboarding tours, dashboards, and task flows — meant every design decision had to work across fundamentally different mental models and goals.

Key Findings — High & Moderate Severity

No onboarding guidance for new artisans
A new artisan completing their profile had no indication of what to do next or what completing their profile unlocks. No progress checklist, no prompt. Significant drop-off risk.

Bio field has no guidance
The bio field was blank with no example or placeholder. Artisans didn't know what to write or how it affects their visibility to customers.
HIGH
GPS error state too alarming on first load
Riders logging in for the first time were immediately confronted with multiple red error messages about location permission — creating unnecessary anxiety before any context was given.
HIGH
Conflicting 'Live' badge when GPS is denied
The header showed a green 'Live' badge even when location permission was denied — a direct contradiction that undermines trust in the system.
HIGH
Artisan dashboard gives no direction to new users
A new artisan with all zeros sees no guidance on what to do. No getting-started checklist, no prompt to complete availability. The dashboard was silent.
MOD
Commission section uses alarming language
'What You Owe (Personal Account Payments)' in orange created a sense of debt even when the balance was zero. Recommended reword: 'Commission Summary'.

Trust signals missing on homepage
The platform's operational history is never mentioned on the homepage — a missed opportunity to differentiate from newer startups and build immediate credibility.

Designing the Onboarding Tours

The single most critical finding from Round 1 was that new artisans and riders had no guidance after signing up. The dashboard showed all zeros with no indication of what to do next — a silent screen that created confusion and drop-off risk.

The solution was a contextual onboarding tour for each user type: a 5-step guided modal for artisans and a 4-step modal for riders. Each tour highlights the actual UI element being explained, includes a progress bar and step counter for orientation, and ends with a motivational close screen. A replayable help button means users can revisit the tour anytime.

Artisan Tour — 5 Steps

Workspace intro → setup checklist → job management → profile & rate → ready to earn

Rider Tour — 4 Steps

Dashboard intro → pickups overview → queue management → you're ready to ride

Why it works: Each modal is anchored to the part of the screen it's explaining — users see the real UI in the background, not an abstract illustration. The 'Skip tour' option respects autonomy, and the help (?) button means no user is permanently lost after dismissing it.

Other Key Design Solutions

Further examples of how Round 1 findings translated directly into platform improvements.

BEFORE→AFTER
Rider GPS: alarming error wall → single friendly prompt
Round 1 found riders were met with multiple red error messages on first load. Round 2 confirmed this was resolved to a single calm message: 'Please allow location access to receive nearby pickups.' Location permission is now also a structured checklist item — part of onboarding rather than a surprise error state.
BEFORE→AFTER
Commission language: 'What You Owe' → 'Commission Summary'
Orange 'What You Owe' framing created anxiety even at zero balance. Reworded to neutral, informational language that tracks earnings without implying debt.

Launch Strategy

Following the usability rounds, I developed a phased go-to-market strategy for Chimreff's Lagos launch — an 8-week plan built around a single core principle.

Core Principle: Supply Before Demand. Recruit artisans and riders first. A single failed job in Lagos creates lasting damage to word-of-mouth. Build a reliable supply base before spending a single kobo on customer acquisition.

Phase 01
Build Your Supply Base
Weeks 1–3
Onboard 30+ active artisans and riders before any customer campaign launches. Field recruitment at Computer Village (Ikeja), Alaba International Market, and AC/appliance repairers in Surulere. WhatsApp community, video testimonials from early sign-ups.
Phase 02
Soft Launch & Stress Test
Weeks 4–5
Complete 30–50 real jobs before public launch. Invite trusted contacts, monitor every job manually, log every issue — bugs, slow response times, artisan no-shows. Fix before going public. Request honest feedback from every test user.
Phase 03
Customer-Side Launch
Weeks 6–8
Acquire first 100 paying customers through TikTok/Instagram Reels (authentic content outperforms polished ads), Meta paid ads targeting Lagos residents aged 22–45, and estate WhatsApp community outreach in Lekki, Ajah, Ikeja GRA, Yaba, and Victoria Island.
Phase 04
Rider Growth & Retention
Ongoing from Week 5
Build a self-sustaining rider network through referral bonuses, monthly leaderboards with cash prizes, and weekly earnings summaries via WhatsApp. Community milestones celebrated to build loyalty at zero cost.

Key metrics tracked weekly: Job Completion Rate (target 90%+), Average Response Time (target under 30 mins), Customer Repeat Rate as PMF signal, Cost Per Booking.

Outcome

Two structured rounds of heuristic evaluation identified and resolved all critical usability risks before launch. The platform moved from a state where new artisans had zero onboarding guidance and riders faced alarming GPS errors, to a launch-ready product with a thorough fix scorecard and a clear backlog for post-launch iteration.

The research directly shaped the go-to-market strategy — the supply-before-demand principle, the phased launch plan, and the weekly KPI framework all emerged from understanding where the platform was fragile and where first impressions were most at risk.
